Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory framework for cosmetics in India is primarily governed by the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940, and the more recent Cosmetics Rules, 2020. These regu lations are overseen by the Central Drugs Standard Control Organization (CDSCO), which handles cosmetic imports and registrations, and State Licensing Authorities (SLAs), which are responsible for issuing manufacturing licenses and monitoring compliance at the state level. The Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) also plays a key role by setting standards for cosmetic ingredients and products. The Cosmet ics (Amendment) Rules, 2025, further decentralized power, empowering SLAs to suspend or cancel licenses for non-compliance, aiming to expedite enforcement and decision-making. A cosmetic manufacturing license, once granted, remains valid permanently, provided a retention fee is paid every five years.
Regulatory EnvironmentObtaining the necessary licenses and adhering to strict compliance standards are critical for operating in the Indian BPC market. Companies intending to manufacture cosmetics must apply for a license (Form COS-5) or a loan license (Form COS-6) from the relevant State Licensing Authority. The process for obtaining a manufacturing license can take approximately forty-five days from the date of application. For imported cosmetics, an Import Registration Certificate (Form 43) must be obtained from the CDSCO before products can be legally distributed in India, a process that requires a fee of USD 250 for each brand. The regulati ons also impose strict limits on heavy metals in cosmetics; for example, permitted colors must not contain more than 2 parts per million (ppm) of arsenic and 20 ppm of lead.
