Regulatory EnvironmentThe primary legislation governing the mining sector in India is the Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act of 1957 (MMDR Act), which places the control of mines and mineral development under the Union government. This act, which has undergone several amendments, most notably in 2015, 2021, and 2025, mandates that all prospecting and mining activities be conducted under licenses or leases issued by the government. The 2015 amendment introduced auctions as the primary method for allocating mineral concessions to enhance transparency. Subsequent amendments have focused on ensuring the continuity of mineral production and facilitating the participation of private exploration agencies. For environmental compliance, projects are categorized, with Category A activities requiring clearance from the Central Ministry of Environment, Forests, and Climate Change. The validity of environmental clearances for mining projects can be up to 30 years.
Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory framework for mining in India also includes several other key acts to ensure environmental protection and social welfare. These include the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986; the Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980; the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974; and the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981. The MMDR Act establishes mechanisms like the District Mineral Funds (DMFs) for sharing mineral wealth with local communities affected by mining operations. A 2025 amendment to the MMDR Act allows captive mines to sell up to 50% of the minerals produced in a year after meeting their end-use requirements and also increases the contribution of lessees to the National Mineral Exploration Trust (now the National Mineral Exploration and Development Trust) from two percent to three percent of the royalty. Non-compliance with environmental regulations can lead to significant penalties, including fines and, in some cases, demolition orders for illegal constructions.
