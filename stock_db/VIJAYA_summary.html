Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory landscape for diagnostic laboratories in India is fragmented and evolving. The primary legislation is the Clinical Establishments (Registration and Regulation) Act of 2010, which aims to standardize facilities and services. However, its adoption is not nationwide, with only 12 states and all Union Territories (except Delhi) having implemented it to date. As health is a state subject in India, many states have their own legislation for regulating clinical establishments. Consequently, there is no single, all-encompassing regulation governing diagnostic labs across the country. This lack of a strong, unified regulatory framework has led to low entry barriers and a wide variation in standards.
Regulatory EnvironmentIn the absence of mandatory, comprehensive regulation, accreditation has emerged as a form of self-regulation for quality assurance. The National Accreditation Board for Testing and Calibration Laboratories (NABL) is the primary accrediting body, but accreditation is voluntary. Out of an estimated 100,000 to 110,000 labs in India, only around 4,000 have NABL accreditation. This highli ghts that a vast majority of laboratories operate without this quality certification. Ancillary regulations that labs must comply with include state-specific Shops and Establishments Acts for operational standards and the Biomedical Waste Management Rules of 2016. Additionall y, diagnostic kits and reagents are classified as 'drugs' under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act of 1940, requiring approval from the Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation (CDSCO).
