Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory landscape for India's commodity derivatives market is primarily governed by the Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI), which took over from the Forward Markets Commission (FMC) in September 2015. This three-ti ered regulatory framework also involves the central government, which formulates policies on exchange recognition and commodity listings, and the exchanges themselves, which develop products in line with SEBI's rules. The Securitie s Contracts (Regulation) Act of 1956 provides the overarching legal framework, giving the government significant oversight over securities trading and stock exchange operations. SEBI's master circular for the commodity derivatives segment, published on August 4, 2023, outlines detailed guidelines for market participants. These regulat ions cover various aspects, including trading hours, daily price limits (which can be up to 4% for certain agricultural commodities), and a preference for physical settlement of contracts over cash settlement.
Regulatory EnvironmentSEBI has been actively refining the regulatory framework to enhance market efficiency and participation. A recent proposal in May 2026 aims to simplify regulations by removing the "Close to the Money" (CTM) option series mechanism, which is considered complex and not in line with international practices. The proposal also suggests reducing the mandatory Product Advisory Committee meetings for non-agricultural commodities from two to one per year. Additionally, on June 19, 2026, SEBI revised the early pay-in mechanism for commodity derivatives, allowing clearing corporations to waive margins on delivery-backed positions based on their risk assessment, with the new rule set to take effect from September 21, 2026. To encourage agricultural sector participation, SEBI has also lowered regulatory fees for agricultural commodity derivatives.
