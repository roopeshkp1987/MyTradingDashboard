Regulatory EnvironmentThe Indian automotive sector is primarily regulated by the Ministry of Road Transport & Highways (MoRTH), which enforces the Motor Vehicles Act of 1988 and the Central Motor Vehicle Rules (CMVR) of 1989. A significa nt recent regulatory development was the nationwide implementation of the Bharat Stage VI (BS-VI) emission norms on April 1, 2020. This was a major leap, skipping the BS-V stage entirely, and mandated a significant reduction in pollutants. BS-VI stand ards require a 70% reduction in Nitrogen Oxide (NOx) for diesel engines and 25% for petrol engines compared to BS-IV. It also dra stically lowered the permissible sulfur content in fuel from 50 parts per million (ppm) to 10 ppm, facilitating cleaner combustion. This transi tion required substantial investment from manufacturers for technological upgrades, leading to an average vehicle price increase of 10-20%.
Regulatory EnvironmentVehicle safety regulations have been significantly enhanced with the introduction of the Bharat New Car Assessment Program (Bharat NCAP), which was launched in August 2023. This voluntar y program provides star ratings based on crash test performance, focusing on Adult Occupant Protection (AOP) and Child Occupant Protection (COP). The tests inc lude a frontal offset impact at 64 km/h, a side impact at 50 km/h, and a pole side impact test at 29 km/h. While partici pation is not mandatory, the program encourages manufacturers to improve safety features beyond the minimum regulatory requirements, such as airbags and ABS, which became standard for all models sold after October 2019. The new draft , Bharat NCAP 2.0, proposes expanding the scope to include tests for vulnerable road user protection, reflecting an evolving focus on comprehensive vehicle safety.
