OverviewCoal India Limited (CIL), a Maharatna Public Sector Undertaking established in 1975, is the single largest coal producer in the world and plays a crucial role in India's energy sector, accounting for approximately 80% of the country's coal production. Headquartered in Kolkata, West Bengal, the company operates 313 mines as of fiscal year 2023-24, which includes 168 opencast mines, 131 underground mines, and 14 mixed mines, spread across eight states in India. The Government of India holds a 63.13% stake in the company as of March 31, 2024. For the fiscal year 2023-24, CIL reported consolidated net sales of ₹1,31,666.69 crore and a profit after tax of ₹37,369.13 crore, underscoring its significant financial performance and scale of operations.
OverviewCIL operates through seven wholly-owned coal-producing subsidiaries and one mine planning and consultancy company, Central Mine Planning & Design Institute (CMPDI). As of April 1, 2020, the company had a workforce of 272,445 employees, making it one of the largest corporate employers in India. In addition to its mining operations, CIL also runs 12 coal washeries, comprising 10 for coking coal and 2 for non-coking coal, to improve the quality of its product. The company has a significant focus on employee development, with 26 training institutes and 84 vocational training centers. In the fiscal year 2019-20, CIL produced 602.138 million tonnes of coal, achieving 91% of its target for the year, demonstrating its vast production capabilities.

Regulatory Risk• The Indian government has amended the Colliery Control Rules, 2004, to streamline the process for opening new coal and lignite mines. P reviously, mine owners needed prior approval from the Coal Controller's Organisation (CCO), a process that could add significant delays. T he Colliery Control (Amendment) Rules, 2025, which took effect on December 23, 2025, empower the board of the coal company to approve the opening of a new mine, seam, or section of a seam. T his change is expected to reduce the time to operationalize a mine by up to two months. H owever, this delegation of authority also places the onus of compliance with all central and state government regulations directly on the company's board. Companies are still required to inform the CCO of the approval within 15 days. T his shift, while intended to improve efficiency, introduces a new layer of direct accountability and potential liability for the company's board of directors.
