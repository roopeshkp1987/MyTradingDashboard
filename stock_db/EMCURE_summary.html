Recent TrendsA significant recent trend is the government's focus on self-reliance through Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes. The PLI scheme for pharmaceuticals, with a budget of ₹15,000 crore, has attracted investments of ₹40,890 crore, far exceeding its six-year target of ₹17,275 crore. A par allel PLI scheme for bulk drugs (APIs) with a ₹6,940 crore budget has also surpassed its investment goals, with ₹4,763.34 crore invested against a target of ₹4,329.95 crore as of September 2025. These schem es have collectively resulted in avoiding imports worth ₹3,591 crore. Specifically, the bulk drug scheme enabled the domestic manufacturing of 26 previously imported APIs, generating sales of ₹2,315.44 crore by September 2025.
Recent TrendsThe Indian pharmaceutical sector is experiencing rapid digitalization, with online pharmacy channels projected to grow at a CAGR of 9.45%, supported by favorable government regulations and integration with the Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission. This contrasts with the traditional retail pharmacies, which still held a majority market share of 64.57% in 2025. There is also a notable shift towards high-value biologics and biosimilars, which is the fastest-growing segment and is projected to make India's domestic biosimilars market worth US$35 billion by 2030. Within therapeu tic areas, oncology is expanding the quickest, with a forecasted CAGR of 8.22% through 2031, driven by the increasing uptake of biosimilars and state-funded cancer reimbursements.
Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory landscape in India is heavily influenced by government price controls aimed at ensuring the affordability of essential medicines. The National Pharmaceutical Pricing Authority (NPPA), established in 1997, is the primary body that fixes and revises the prices of controlled bulk drugs and formulations. Its key instrum ents are the Drug Price Control Order (DPCO) and the National List of Essential Medicines (NLEM). The government recently expanded the number of drugs under price control from 74 to 348, which is expected to cover up to 30% of all drugs sold in the country. For pharmaceuti cal companies, the inclusion of a drug in the NLEM can have a significant financial impact, potentially reducing ceiling prices by up to 25% and compressing gross margins by 200 to 400 basis points.
Regulatory EnvironmentIndia is strengthening its regulatory framework to align with stringent international standards, enhancing the global credibility of its pharmaceutical products. A key development is the revision of Schedule M of the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940, which mandates Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) to bring them on par with global benchmarks set by the WHO and EU. These 2025 upda tes introduce advanced concepts like Pharmaceutical Quality System (PQS) and Quality Risk Management (QRM). The Central Dru gs Standard Control Organization (CDSCO) is also increasing its oversight through initiatives such as mandatory QR codes on certain drugs and the launch of a system to track high-risk solvents. Furthermore, th e New Drugs and Clinical Trials (NDCT) Rules of 2019 have streamlined the approval process, including provisions for waiving local clinical trials for drugs already approved by major global regulatory bodies like the US FDA and EMA.
