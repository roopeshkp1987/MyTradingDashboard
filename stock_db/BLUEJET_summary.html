Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory framework for India's pharmaceutical industry is primarily managed by the Central Drugs Standard Control Organization (CDSCO) and state-level authorities, under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act of 1940 and its subsequent rules. A signifi cant recent update was the implementation of the New Drugs and Clinical Trial (NDCT) Rules in March 2019, which replaced Part XA and Schedule Y of the 1945 rules to streamline clinical research and align with global Good Clinical Practices (GCP). Obtaining a manufacturing license is a multi-step process that requires approvals from the State Drug Control Department and involves submitting detailed documentation, including factory layout plans and technical staff qualifications, followed by a physical inspection of the premises. For new d rugs, a specific permission under Form CT-11 is mandatory before manufacturing for clinical trials or bioequivalence studies can commence.
Regulatory EnvironmentGovernment policies significantly impact the pharmaceutical sector's dynamics, focusing on affordability, innovation, and domestic manufacturing. The Drug Price Control Order (DPCO), first introduced in 1970 and enforced by the National Pharmaceutical Pricing Authority (NPPA) established in 1994, regulates the prices of essential bulk drugs and their formulations. The Patent Act of 1970 was amended in 2005 to align with the WTO's TRIPS agreement, shifting from process patents to product patents. To encourag e R&D, the government offers a weighted tax deduction of 200% on R&D expenditure and has launched initiatives like the "New Millennium Indian Technology Leadership Initiative". These polic ies, combined with a corporate tax rate of 22% for existing companies and 15% for new manufacturing firms, aim to balance patient affordability with industry growth and competitiveness in the global market.
