Sectors/IndustriesThe Non-Banking Financial Company (NBFC) sector in India, where IFCI operates as a term-lending institution, demonstrates significant growth and systemic importance. The sector's total balance sheet stood at ₹61.1 trillion in FY25 and is projected to reach ₹92.9 trillion by FY28, growing at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of approximately 15%. This outpaces the growth of scheduled commercial banks, which saw 11.5% growth in FY25 compared to the NBFC sector's 19.4%. The total assets under management (AUM) for NBFCs increased from under ₹2,00,000 crore in the year 2000 to ₹43,00,000 crore by the end of FY24. This rapid expansion highlights the sector's critical role in financial intermediation, particularly in serving retail and MSME segments, which are often underserved by traditional banks.
Sectors/IndustriesInfrastructure financing remains a key segment for term-lending institutions, driven by substantial government focus and investment. India's government plans to spend $1.4 trillion on infrastructure between 2019 and 2023, with a target of achieving a $5 trillion economy by 2025. The government's National Infrastructure Pipeline (NIP) further outlines this massive undertaking, reinforcing the long-term demand for project finance.
Recent TrendsThe Indian NBFC sector is undergoing a significant digital transformation, fundamentally altering credit assessment and delivery. The adoption of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) allows for loan processing in minutes rather than days, with the Indian FinTech market projected to reach a size of USD 150 billion by 2027. This "intelligent lending" trend leverages alternative data sources, expanding credit access to previously underserved segments. A notable shift is occurring towards secured lending following regulatory changes in 2023-24; between June 2023 and June 2025, the proportion of very low-risk borrowers in NBFC-fintech portfolios rose from 20% to nearly 29%, while high-risk borrowers decreased from approximately 38% to under 29%.
Regulatory EnvironmentThe Reserve Bank of India (RBI) has implemented a comprehensive Scale-Based Regulation (SBR) framework, creating a tiered supervisory system for NBFCs. This framework classifies NBFCs into four layers: Base Layer (NBFC-BL) for assets under ₹1,000 crore, Middle Layer (NBFC-ML) for assets up to ₹1 lakh crore, Upper Layer (NBFC-UL) for the largest entities, and a Top Layer (NBFC-TL) that is typically kept empty. This structure imposes regulations that are commensurate with the size and systemic risk of the institution. For instance, Middle and Upper Layer NBFCs must maintain a minimum Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets Ratio (CRAR) of 15%, with Tier-I capital being no less than 10%, ensuring robust financial health and stability within the sector.
Regulatory EnvironmentThe RBI has instituted specific prudential norms to govern lending practices and manage risk exposure for NBFCs. For Middle Layer NBFCs, exposure to a single borrower is capped at 25% of their Tier-I capital, a rule designed to prevent concentration risk. Furthermore, to regulate capital market exposure, financing for Initial Public Offerings (IPOs) is limited to a maximum of ₹1 crore per individual borrower. In a move to streamline regulations, the RBI introduced a new category in 2026, the "Unregistered Type I NBFC," which exempts entities with assets below ₹1,000 crore that do not use public funds and have no customer interface from mandatory registration, thereby reducing the compliance burden on smaller, low-risk players in the sector.
