Regulated by the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (CERC) since June 27, 2008, the company has been publicly listed on the BSE and NSE since October 2017. For the fiscal year 2025, IEX reported a significant trading volume of 121 Billion Units (BU), marking an 18.7% year-on-year growth. The company's consolidated financial performance for FY2025 included a total revenue of Rs 657.4 crore and a net profit (PAT) of Rs 429.2 crore, with an EBITDA of Rs 588.4 crore, underscoring its central role in India's power sector through efficient price discovery.

For fiscal year 2025, transaction fees constituted 78.7% of revenue, with other income (including investments) at 18.1% and admission & annual fees at 3.2%. The company's primary offerings include a Day-Ahead Market (DAM), launched in 2008, and a Term-Ahead Market (TAM) introduced in 2009. It also facilitates trading in Renewable Energy Certificates (REC) and Energy Saving Certificates (ESCerts). To diversify, IEX established a wholly-owned subsidiary, the Indian Gas Exchange (IGX), which provides a trading platform for spot and forward contracts in natural gas and serves over 500 registered clients.

This technological backbone successfully handled a total trading volume of 121 Billion Units (BU) during the 2025 fiscal year, demonstrating its capability to manage substantial transaction loads. Regulatory RiskThe primary regulatory risk for Indian Energy Exchange (IEX) stems from the Central Electricity Regulatory Commission's (CERC) power market regulations of 2021 and the recent directive to implement market coupling. This reform, set to begin in January 2026, will pool buy-and-sell orders from all three national power exchanges (IEX, PXIL, and HPX) to determine a single, uniform clearing price. This negates IEX's key advantage of setting the market price due to its superior liquidity. The formal announcement of this policy on July 24th precipitated a significant market reaction, highlighting the investment risk associated with regulatory shifts. Further, any adverse revision by the CERC to the current transaction fee, which stands at ₹0.02/kWh, could directly and negatively impact profitability.
Market RiskIEX faces substantial market risk from intensifying competition and pricing pressure following the introduction of market coupling. The company's historical dominance, evidenced by an 84% market share as of FY24 and a 99% share in the Day-Ahead-Market (DAM), was built on its liquidity advantage, which is now neutralized. This regulatory change enables competitors like PXIL and HPX to compete on an equal footing, primarily on transaction fees. Price discovery will no longer be an IEX monopoly; for instance, in FY24, the per-unit price on IEX was ₹5.16, compared to ₹9.71 on PXIL, a discrepancy that market coupling will eliminate. This has impacted investor confidence, with the stock falling over 65% from its 2021 peak and its price-to-earnings ratio contracting from over 100 to approximately 24.
Business RiskA key business risk for IEX is its high dependency on transaction fees for revenue, making it vulnerable to both volume migration and fee reductions. While the short-term power market is expanding, growing at a CAGR of 18% and increasing its share of total electricity generation from under 10% in FY20 to around 13% currently, IEX’s ability to capture this growth is now challenged. The introduction of market coupling means smaller exchanges can attract volume by offering lower fees, directly pressuring IEX's revenue and margins. Although overall power demand is projected to grow at a CAGR of 6% to 8%, and exchange-transacted volumes have grown at a 29% CAGR from FY09 to FY23, IEX’s future profitability is at risk if it cannot retain its transaction volumes or is forced into a price war with competitors.
