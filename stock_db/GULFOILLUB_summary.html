Recent TrendsA significant trend in the Indian lubricants market is the shift towards synthetic and high-performance lubricants, driven by the implementation of Bharat Stage VI (BS-VI) emission norms. These regulations necessitate the use of low-SAPS (sulfated ash, phosphorus, and sulfur) lubricants to protect advanced after-treatment systems in modern engines. This has led to a premiumization of the market, with manufacturers like Castrol India launching specialized products to meet these new standards. The electric vehicle (EV) market is also influencing the industry, with EV registrations reaching 1.97 million units in FY 2024-25, creating demand for specialized fluids such as dielectric coolants and e-transmission fluids. Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory landscape for the Indian lubricant industry is evolving, with a key development being the implementation of Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) for used oils, effective from April 1, 2024. This policy mandates that producers are accountable for the collection and environmentally sound disposal of used lubricants, promoting recycling and a circular economy. The EPR targets for producers and importers of base and lubrication oils start at 10% and are set to incrementally increase. All entities involved, including producers, importers, collection agents, and recyclers, are required to register on the Central Pollution Control Board's (CPCB) portal. This framework aims to formalize the largely fragmented and informal used oil recycling ecosystem, which currently sees only a fraction of the approximately 1.3 million tonnes of used lubricating oil generated annually recovered through formal channels.
Regulatory EnvironmentThe Bureau of Indian Standards (BIS) plays a crucial role in regulating the quality of lubricants in India. All lubricating and specialty oils are required to meet the relevant Indian Standards notified by the BIS. Manufacturers must obtain consent clearance under the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1977, and the Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981, as well as authorization under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, to apply for the ECOMARK label. Furthermore, businesses involved in the processing, manufacturing, blending, packaging, refining, or sale of lubricating oils and greases must obtain a LUBE License from the Ministry of Petroleum and Natural Gas to ensure adherence to quality and safety standards. For re-refined lubricating oils to be considered environmentally friendly, they must contain more than 50% by volume of re-refined products.
