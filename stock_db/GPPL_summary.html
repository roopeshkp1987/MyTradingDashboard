The facility's current annual handling capacities are significant, with the container terminal capable of processing 1.35 million TEUs, the dry bulk terminal managing 4-5 million metric tons, the liquid cargo terminal having a capacity of 2 million metric tons, and the RoRo (Roll-on/Roll-off) yard accommodating 250,000 vehicles. The port operates under a 30-year concession agreement with the Gujarat Maritime Board, which started in September 1998. The company employs approximately 455 people.

Sectors/IndustriesThe Indian maritime sector is a critical component of the nation's economy, handling approximately 95% of international trade by volume and 77% by value. The industry's structure includes around 12 major ports and over 187 non-major ports, which are governed by central and state authorities, respectively. The India maritime freight market was valued at USD 36.2 billion in 2025 and is forecast to expand to USD 51.0 billion by 2034, reflecting a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 3.72%. In fiscal year 2025, India's major ports handled approximately 855 million tonnes of cargo, marking a 4.3% increase from the previous year. This growth is supported by significant government initiatives aimed at modernizing infrastructure and enhancing capacity to meet rising trade demands.
Recent TrendsA primary trend in the Indian maritime industry is the aggressive push towards infrastructure modernization and capacity expansion, driven by government programs like Sagarmala. Under this initiative, 567 projects have been identified for development with an estimated investment of INR 59,000 crore. This focus complements the growing trend of containerization and the development of multimodal logistics parks to improve efficiency. For instance, in the first nine months of FY26, India's container shipment trade grew to nearly 11.01 million TEUs, an 8.42% increase year-over-year. Another key trend is the increasing utilization of inland waterways, where cargo traffic has surged from 18.07 million metric tonnes (MMT) in 2013-14 to 146 MMT in 2024-25, demonstrating a shift towards a more integrated logistics network.
Regulatory EnvironmentThe regulatory framework for India's port sector has been significantly updated with the introduction of the Indian Ports Act, 2025, which replaced the archaic Indian Ports Act of 1908. This new act aims to streamline governance, promote ease of doing business, and foster integrated development across the nation's 12 major and 213 non-major ports (as of March 2025). It grants statutory recognition to the Maritime State Development Council (MSDC) to enhance coordination between central and state governments. Following this, the Draft Indian Port Rules, 2026, propose to establish common standards for all ports, unifying regulations that previously differed between major and non-major ports, and mandating audits every two years to ensure compliance and accountability.
